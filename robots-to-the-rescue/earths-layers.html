Earthquakes
Tectonic Plates
The Earth's crust is made up of plates, and where the plates meet are cracks or faults.
During earthquakes, the ground moves along these faults.
Tsunamis
While wind generates most ocean waves, the movement of the ocean floor can cause a tsunami. Tsunamis are triggered by underwater earthquakes, landslides, or volcanic activity.

The wave grows in height as it moves toward the shore.
Floods
Floods occur when there is too much water for the ground to absorb.

Flooding can be caused by too much rain that cannot be drained away quickly enough.
Tornadoes
Tornadoes are narrow funnels of wind that can blow at over 300 mph.

They occur and disappear relatively quickly, but they can leave tremendous damage in their path.
Hurricanes
Hurricanes are big storms. They have powerful winds that rotate around a region of calm, called the "eye." The effects of hurricanes can produce high winds, storm surges, intense rainfall and flooding.
Crust
The crust is where we live. It's the outer layer of Earth: a thin, solid layer of rock. It is divided into tectonic plates that move above the mantle.

When these plates move away or toward each other, or when they scrape against each other, they can cause earthquakes, tsunamis, and volcanic eruptions.

The mantle is hot and semi-solid. The rocks rise from the heat and then cool and sink back down. This creates a motion much like what is seen inside a lava lamp. This motion is called convection.
Mantle
Convection causes Earth's tectonic plates to move. These movements can cause earthquakes, tsunamis, and volcanic eruptions. Volcanoes can form and erupt when activity in the mantle causes magma to rise up through Earth's crust!
