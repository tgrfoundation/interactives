Your last stop in the Zoo Lab is the DNA Room. The DNA Room is your most important stop in this investigation.
Short for deoxyribonucleic acid, the DNA in an animal's cells is what makes an animal unique. DNA exists in the nucleus of every animal's cell. Within the DNA are genes that influence how an animal grows and develops.

DNA is made up of molecules called nucleotides. There are four common types of nucleotides: Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Guanine (G) and Cytosine (C). A single strand of DNA is made up of a unique combination of these four letters.

Mutations, or changes, to DNA can happen when DNA is not copied perfectly or, in rarer cases, due to environmental factors. Mutations can be beneficial, harmful or even non-impactful. The differences and similarities among animals' DNA is a strong indicator of the degree by which animals are related.

To see how closely related one species is to another, you can examine which animals' DNA strands are the most similar. You'll use a technique called gel electrophoresis to separate and study the DNA. During this process, restriction enzymes are added to DNA, which help break the DNA into smaller segments.
DNA is colored with dye and poured into a box filled with gel and a buffer that conducts a current.
An electrical current is turned on that pulls the DNA through the gel towards the positive side of the box.
DNA is spread over the gel, ending the process. The DNA is ready to study.
