What are some ways to estimate the vast distances between Earth and the planets, moons, and stars scientists study? One method is parallax.
Spacecraft use parallax to estimate distances. You can use parallax to estimate the distance to faraway objects.
1
Hold your thumb out in front of you.
2
Close one of your eyes, and note the object in the distance.
3
Open your eye, and look back at your thumb.

Keep opening and closing one eye at a time and focusing on your thumb and the object in the distance.

What do you notice?
4
Now, move your thumb closer to your face and again focus on the distant object. Then your thumb with different eyes, one at a time.
What do you notice about the distance and your thumb? The amount that the object seems to move is related to how far away the object is. This is parallax!
Absorption: Absorption occurs when radiated energy is absorbed by an object. Absorption spectra are produced when the light emitted from a hot, dense material passes through a cooler, less dense gas.
Emission: Emission indicates that radiation is being released by an object.
Astronomers can tell a lot about celestial objects through a technique called spectrometry. Looking at the spectrum of visible light and other forms of electromagnetic radiation, spectrometry can reveal the chemical composition of stars, planets, galaxies and other objects found in space, just like how a prism can reveal all the colors that make up light!
Astronomical spectrometry is the study of how electromagnetic waves interact with matter, including the absorption and emission of radiation.

Stars, planets, and other celestial objects produce an emission spectrum or an absorption spectrum. Each spectrum can then be compared with the spectra of known substances.
The lines on each spectrum correspond to the wavelengths of radiation either being absorbed or emitted.
Astronomical spectrometry helps scientists classify unknown samples. This will be critical to your mission. Let's see what you can identify using astronomical spectrometry!
